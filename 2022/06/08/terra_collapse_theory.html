My Terra Collapse Theory

This is just my theory of what happened to Terra given the data I have seen from different locations, presented as best as possible at a beginner level.

TL;DR

Four large sell events in less than 24 hours (over a weekend) led to UST losing it’s $1 peg, which led to a sell-off in both UST and LUNA.

Terra Collapse Explained

My intention with this autopsy is to understand what went wrong with Terra. I had a very small amount of tokens on the Terra blockchain as a way to test its possibilities before hopefully making a more substantial investment. Lucky for me, I had not made that major investment before the crash. I want to do this autopsy to figure out what I did not see ahead of time so I don’t make the same mistake on another chain.

What is Terra?

Terra is a Layer 1 smart contract blockchain. This makes it similar to Ethereum and Solana, which both also host smart contract applications, but different from Bitcoin or Dogecoin, which both only have one application, which is to transfer value between addresses. With Terra, much like Ethereum or Solana users are able to write applications, which are published to their respective blockchain to be executed by users of that application. What makes Terra distinct from Ethereum or Solana is that, along with its native token LUNA, Terra has a stablecoin (named Terra USD, or UST for short) that is managed as part of its base protocol. Ethereum and Solana both have stablecoins which can be used on their blockchains, but are simply applications written and managed by separate entities.

How does Terra work?

As mentioned above, the unique proposition for Terra is that it’s native token LUNA and its stablecoin UST are managed as part of the base protocol. What this means in practice is that you can always turn one dollar worth of LUNA into one UST, or vice versa, turn one UST into one dollar worth of LUNA. Theoretically what this means is if the value of UST were ever to fall below $1, then LUNA holders would swoop in, and convert their LUNA to UST at $1 and the value would return to the peg. This economic exchange worked perfectly as intended as long as their was demand for LUNA such as you see in a crypto bull market. Once we entered a bear market and a disproportionate number of users wanted UST instead of LUNA the economic model broke down quickly.

Expansion of Terra Assets outside the Terra Ecosystem

As the Terra ecosystem grew in value, the organization responsible for its development, TerraForm Labs (TFL) began expanding its usefulness outside of simply trading within the Terra Ecosystem. UST was added to Curve finance in order to trade it with other stablecoins. BTC and AVAX (the Avalanche ecosystems coin) were added to the reserves backing UST along with LUNA, with the objective of increasing its stability. This expansion outside the Terra ecosystem sowed the seeds for its eventual collapse.

Where it broke down

In early May 3 major selling events for UST coincided (weather intentionally or coincidentally) to cause a de-peg from $1. TFL intended to set up a new pool on Curve finance in order to grow USTs dominance. As part of this expansion, on May 7, the LFG withdrew roughly $150M worth of UST from Curve. As soon as they did this, an anonymous whale swapped $85M worth of UST for USDC. Another $200M was simultaneously sold on Binance for USDC. The following morning, as part of the TFL’s BTC purchase, the hedge fund that had facilitated the purchase, Three Arrows Capital (3AC) sold a large portion of their UST from the BTC exchange. These four events, LFG withdraw from Curve Finance, two anonymous traders exchanging USt for USDC, and 3AC selling their UST holdings, all happening in less than 24 hours caused a sharp de-peg from $1 to roughly 75 cents.

Things go from bad to worse

Remember that a feature of the Terra Ecosystem is that LUNA can be exchanged for UST at a rate of $1. Once the de-peg started crypto traders from outside the Terra Ecosystem could trade their coin for UST at 75 cents on the dollar, then burn that UST for $1 worth of LUNA, then sell their LUNA for another coin (such as BTC or ETH). This further increased the number of LUNA coins right at the same time that the value of LUNA coins was decreasing due to concerns over the de-peg. On May 9th the entire supply of LUNA became worth less than the entire supply of UST, which meant you could no longer be guaranteed a 1:1 conversion, and at that point LUNA was effectively a failed cryptocurrency.

What did I miss

In my analysis of Terra before buying in, I felt confident that trading bots which would profit from a temporary de-peg would have a sufficient profit motive to maintain the $1 peg. I did not understand how a large transfer of funds in a short period of time (malicious or coincidental) could cause a significant de-peg event. I also underestimated the psychological effect a de-peg would have on investors and how quickly they would abandon UST in the event of a de-peg event.
